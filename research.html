Theme 1

AI-Mediated Coordination in Multi-Party Social Dilemmas

Most human-AI collaboration research studies one person working with one system. I focus on the harder case: several actors share responsibility for an outcome, each has incomplete information, and each can benefit by letting someone else bear the cost.

Global supply chains make these tensions concrete. Buyers must decide whether to fund safer production, investigate warning signs, remediate harm, or leave a supplier. My simulation brings those choices into one repeated game so I can isolate where cooperation fails and test specific roles for AI intervention.

AI-Mediated Coordination in Multi-Party Supply Chain Social Dilemmas

Capstone Research Project, NYU Abu Dhabi · 2026–Present · Advisors: Prof. Hanan Salam, Prof. Benjamin Rosche

I built a calibrated environment with five buyers and three suppliers interacting over repeated rounds. Buyer behavior follows a quantal-response model with a belief solver linking support, monitoring, and action choices. Across a 12-condition sweep, the baseline reproduces the intended inefficient equilibrium: roughly 22–26% welfare loss relative to the cooperative ceiling, near-universal supplier defection, and little remediation.

Next, I am turning the simulation into an experimental platform. The central comparison is between the algorithmic baseline and human sessions in which AI serves as an advisor, coordinator, monitor, or commitment-support system. The aim is not only to ask whether AI improves welfare, but to identify why.

Capstone seminar paper available on request.

Theme 2

Representation Learning in Scientific Domains

Protein language models learn from sequence at enormous scale, but adapting them can be expensive and biologically agnostic. I study whether known biological structure—such as evolutionary substitution patterns and physicochemical distance—can provide a better starting point for efficient fine-tuning.

Bio-LoRA: Biological Priors as Inductive Bias for Low-Rank Adaptation

Research Assistant, eBRAIN Lab, NYU Abu Dhabi · 2026–Present · Co-first author

I co-developed Bio-LoRA, which initializes ESM-2 LoRA adapters with BLOSUM62 or Grantham priors instead of a generic random subspace. We evaluate the method on mutation effects, protein stability, and signal peptides.

Bio-LoRA improves on standard LoRA without changing inference cost. At the 650M scale, the BLOSUM62 variant outperforms both LoRA and full fine-tuning on signal-peptide MCC and cleavage-site exactness; on mutation effects, it improves Spearman correlation from 0.434 to 0.453. Multi-seed experiments remain central because several apparent single-run gains do not replicate.

Submitted to an EMNLP 2026 workshop. Manuscript available on request.

Theme 3

Multilingual and Code-Mixed NLP

Code-mixed language is a useful stress test for multilingual models: it is common in everyday speech, scarce in standard benchmarks, and difficult to represent with a single language assumption. I compare task-specific fine-tuning with zero-shot prompting under the same entity-level evaluation.

Hinglish Named Entity Recognition Benchmark

Independent Research Project · Fall 2024

I fine-tuned mBERT and XLM-RoBERTa on COMI-LINGUA for Hindi-English named entity recognition. The best model reached 78% entity-level F1 and outperformed zero-shot GPT-4o and LLaMA 3.1 under the same evaluation, showing that a smaller specialized model can remain the better tool for a narrow, low-resource task.

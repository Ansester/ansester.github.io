Research Agenda

Research

My main interest is cooperative AI: how artificial agents change cooperation and coordination in groups that include both people and machines. I also work on protein models and multilingual NLP. Those projects are interesting on their own, and they keep me focused on the same basic standard: a result should be convincing, repeatable, and useful outside the exact setting where it was first measured.

In Depth

Themes

Theme 1

AI-Mediated Coordination in Multi-Party Social Dilemmas

How do artificial agents affect cooperation in groups where responsibility is spread across many actors? A lot of human-AI collaboration research studies one person working with one AI system. Many real coordination problems are messier: several actors share responsibility for a common outcome, while each actor has a private reason to free-ride. I study whether AI agents can change that dynamic when they serve as advisors, coordinators, monitors, or commitment-support systems.

My current work builds a simulation-based research platform around global supply chains. Supply chains are a useful setting because they bring several problems together: public-goods support for ethical production, strategic ignorance under noisy monitoring, threshold coordination on remediation, and exit versus engagement after misconduct. Put together, these mechanisms create a world where individually reasonable choices can produce worse outcomes for everyone, and where AI intervention may have room to help.

AI-Mediated Coordination in Multi-Party Supply Chain Social Dilemmas

Capstone Research Project, NYU Abu Dhabi · 2026–Present · Advisors: Prof. Hanan Salam, Prof. Benjamin Rosche

Built a calibrated game-theoretic simulation with five buyers and three suppliers over repeated rounds. The model combines public goods support, strategic ignorance, threshold remediation, exit versus engagement, and auditing in one domain-grounded environment. Buyer policy uses quantal response equilibrium with a Stage 2 belief solver that jointly fixes support, monitoring, and action probabilities. The simulation reproduces the target inefficient-equilibrium pattern, with about 22-26% welfare loss versus the cooperative ceiling, about 99% supplier defection, and about 7% remediation across a 12-cell calibration sweep with 50 simulations per cell. The backend has 101 passing tests and has gone through three audit rounds.

The next phase turns the simulation into an experimental platform. I will compare the algorithmic baseline against human-participant sessions where AI agents act as advisors, coordinators, monitors, or commitment-support systems. The longer-term question is whether AI mediation reduces welfare loss in real multi-party settings, and whether the mechanism is better information sharing, stronger commitments, clearer coordination, redesigned incentives, or some mix of these.

Capstone seminar paper available on request.

Theme 2

Representation Learning in Scientific Domains

Large pretrained models are now common feature extractors in scientific work, but adapting them to specific tasks can be expensive and disconnected from biological knowledge. I am interested in how domain structure such as substitution patterns, physicochemical constraints, and evolutionary priors can be added to parameter-efficient fine-tuning. I am just as interested in the evaluation question: when does that extra structure actually help, and when does it only look good in one run?

Bio-LoRA: Biological Priors as Inductive Bias for Low-Rank Adaptation

Research Assistant, eBRAIN Lab, NYU Abu Dhabi · 2026–Present · Co-first author

Co-developed Bio-LoRA, a parameter-efficient fine-tuning method that initializes LoRA adapters for ESM-2 protein models with residue-level biological priors: evolutionary substitution matrices (BLOSUM62) and physicochemical distance (Grantham), instead of a generic random subspace. We evaluated it on mutation-effect prediction, protein-stability regression, and signal-peptide prediction.

Bio-LoRA improves over standard LoRA without adding memory or runtime cost. On signal-peptide prediction at the 650M scale, BLOSUM62-guided Bio-LoRA beats both LoRA and full fine-tuning on MCC and cleavage-site exactness. On mutation-effect prediction, it raises Spearman correlation from 0.434 to 0.453 over the LoRA baseline. Because the prior only affects initialization, per-sample memory and runtime match plain LoRA and stay several-fold lower than full fine-tuning.

Submitted to an EMNLP 2026 workshop. Manuscript available on request.

Theme 3

Multilingual and Code-Mixed NLP

Large multilingual models often do well on resource-rich languages but drop off in code-mixed and low-resource settings. That matters in practice, and it also raises a measurement problem. I am interested in how task-specific fine-tuning compares with large-model prompting when the language itself is genuinely mixed.

Hinglish Named Entity Recognition Benchmark

Independent Research Project · Fall 2024

Fine-tuned multilingual transformers, mBERT and XLM-RoBERTa, on the COMI-LINGUA dataset for Hindi-English code-mixed NER. The best model reached 78% entity-level F1, outperforming zero-shot GPT-4o and LLaMA 3.1 baselines under controlled evaluation. I analyzed trade-offs between model scale, task-specific fine-tuning, generalization, and computational cost.

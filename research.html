Computational Experimentation

Evaluating AI Interventions in Social Systems

This project uses an online experiment to identify how different forms of AI support affect human behavior. The central methodological problem is to separate the effect of providing information from the effects of interpreting that information, framing responsibility, and supporting coordination.

Global supply chains provide a setting in which these mechanisms can be measured through repeated decisions. Several buyers jointly shape the conditions under which a supplier can meet labor standards, while each buyer has an incentive to reduce its own costs and rely on others to act. The experiment converts these tensions into observable choices, messages, information links, and group outcomes.

AI and Human Collaboration in Social Dilemmas: Strategic Interpretation, Normative Interpretation, and Collective Action in Shared Supply Chains

With Benjamin Rosche and Hanan Salam. Working paper, June 2026.

We develop an online experiment in which five human buyers repeatedly source from one supplier governed by a fixed decision rule. Buyers decide whether to pay fairly, audit the supplier, form information-sharing links, communicate with other buyers, and join a costly group remediation effort. The game combines public-good underinvestment, strategic ignorance, shared-liability information links, threshold coordination, and negative externalities from co-buyer defection.

The between-groups design compares four conditions:

  1. Structured-information control: a common interface presents game rules, track records, audit findings, and communication tools without AI interpretation.
  2. Strategic interpretation AI: a private assistant identifies the strategic structure of the participant's current decision without recommending an action.
  3. Normative interpretation AI: the assistant adds frames concerning responsibility, complicity, and the distribution of harm.
  4. Collective action AI: the system recommends potential partners, drafts conditional messages, and tracks tentative coalition commitments.

The AI systems do not choose on behalf of participants. The experiment measures fair pay, auditing, connection formation, remediation, welfare, responsibility attribution, and behavioral convergence. A subsequent withdrawal phase tests whether AI support produces persistent learning or dependence on the interpretive and coordinative scaffold.

Working paper complete and available on request. Experimental implementation is ongoing.

Model Training and Evaluation

Efficient Adaptation of Pretrained Models

This project provides a technically structured setting for studying parameter-efficient fine-tuning. The core questions concern how prior information can be introduced into a pretrained model, how adaptation quality should be measured, and whether performance gains remain stable across model scales and random seeds.

Bio-Informed LoRA for Signal Peptide Prediction

Research Assistant, eBRAIN Lab, NYU Abu Dhabi · 2026 to Present · Co-first author

I co-developed a method that incorporates BLOSUM62, hydrophobicity, and Grantham priors into LoRA adapters for ESM-2 signal peptide prediction. BLOSUM62-guided adaptation matches or exceeds full fine-tuning on SignalP6 benchmarks while training approximately 3.6% of model parameters and using approximately 43% less peak GPU memory at the 3B-parameter scale.

My contribution focuses on the training and evaluation process. I designed multi-seed protocols and a one-factor sensitivity analysis to distinguish stable improvements from results that depend on a particular initialization. Several gains observed under a single random seed did not replicate, demonstrating the importance of variance estimation and controlled comparison.

Under review at an EMNLP 2026 workshop. Manuscript available on request.

Data and Representation

Learning from Code-Mixed Language Data

This project examined how different model classes represent and extract information from a dataset in which languages, scripts, and linguistic contexts are mixed. It also provided a controlled comparison between task-specific fine-tuning and zero-shot use of larger general-purpose models.

Hinglish Named Entity Recognition Benchmark

Research Project · Fall 2024

I fine-tuned mBERT and XLM-RoBERTa on the COMI-LINGUA dataset and evaluated them against zero-shot GPT-4o and LLaMA 3.1 baselines. The best fine-tuned model achieved 78% entity-level F1 under a common evaluation protocol.
